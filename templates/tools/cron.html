    Understanding the fields

    Five fields, one line

    A classic cron schedule is five space-separated fields — minute, hour, day of month, month, day of week — read left to right: */15 2 * * 1-5 means every 15th minute, of hour 2, any date, any month, Monday–Friday. Six- or seven-field expressions with a seconds column are Quartz/Spring extensions, not classic cron, and this page doesn't parse them.

    Minute (0–59)

    The first field picks minutes within the hour. 0 fires on the hour, */15 every quarter hour, 5/10 at :05, :15, :25… (start at 5, step by 10). Cron's resolution stops here — there are no seconds.

    Hour (0–23)

    The second field uses the 24-hour clock: 0 is midnight, 14 is 2 PM. 9-17 covers the working day. The run times this page projects use your browser's local clock — the same expression on a server in another timezone fires at different wall-clock moments.

    Day of month (1–31)

    The third field picks calendar dates. A date a month doesn't have is simply skipped — 0 0 31 * * quietly runs only in the seven 31-day months, and 0 0 29 2 * waits for leap years. Beware the interaction with day of week (see the trap below).

    Month (1–12 or JAN–DEC)

    The fourth field accepts numbers (1 = January) or three-letter names, case-insensitive: JAN, jun, DEC. Ranges and lists work with names too — MAR-MAY, JAN,JUL.

    Day of week (0–7 or SUN–SAT)

    The fifth field counts from Sunday: 0 is Sunday, 1 is Monday … 6 is Saturday — and most crons accept 7 as Sunday too (this page does). Three-letter names work: MON-FRI, SAT,SUN. Weekday ranges don't wrap around the week, so write FRI-SUN as FRI,SAT,SUN or 0,5,6.

    Steps, ranges & lists

    Every field accepts a number, a range a-b (inclusive), a step */n (every n-th value), a stepped range 9-17/2, a bare start-with-step 5/10 (from 5 to the field's maximum, by 10), and comma-separated lists mixing all of the above — 0,30 8-11,13-16 * * MON,WED,FRI is perfectly legal.

    The day-of-month / day-of-week trap

    When both day fields are restricted, cron runs when either one matches — not both. 0 0 13 * FRI fires on every Friday and on the 13th of every month, not just on Friday the 13th. The OR only applies when neither field starts with *; with a star (even a stepped one like */2) the two fields must both match.

    @shortcuts & quirks

    Most crons accept @hourly, @daily (= @midnight), @weekly, @monthly and @yearly (= @annually) as shorthand for the five-field forms — this page expands them. @reboot runs once at boot and has no calendar schedule at all. Around daylight-saving jumps, runs in the skipped or repeated hour shift with your system clock's rules — classic cron is similarly fuzzy there.
