Understanding timestamps

What's an epoch?

Unix time counts seconds since 1970-01-01 00:00:00 UTC — "the epoch", an arbitrary zero chosen by early Unix developers. It's timezone-free by definition: 1,781,234,567 means the same instant in Tokyo and Toronto; only the rendering into a wall-clock date needs a zone. Negative values reach before 1970.

Seconds vs milliseconds (vs µs and ns)

Unix tools speak seconds (10 digits today), JavaScript's Date.now() speaks milliseconds (13), and logging systems often emit micro- or nanoseconds (16, 19). Mixing them up is the classic bug: a seconds value read as milliseconds lands in January 1970; milliseconds read as seconds lands fifty-thousand years out. This page guesses by magnitude and always tells you which unit it assumed.

ISO 8601

2026-06-12T15:30:00Z: biggest unit first, which makes string-sorting equal date-sorting. The T separates date and time; the suffix names the zone — Z is UTC ("Zulu"), +05:30 an offset. No suffix means ambiguous: this page assumes your local clock and says so. A bare date like 2026-06-12 is treated as midnight UTC, matching the standard's (and JavaScript's) reading.

RFC 2822

Thu, 12 Jun 2026 15:30:00 -0400 — the email date format, inherited by HTTP headers and RSS. Human-friendly day names, but English-only and unsortable as text; it survives because billions of Date: headers already speak it.

UTC vs your clock

UTC is the reference everyone offsets from. The robust pattern is: store and exchange UTC (or epoch), convert to local only at the edges — screens and parsers. The moment two systems exchange wall-clock strings without zones, someone's meeting moves three hours.

Time zones are politics

Offsets are law, not physics — countries change them with months of notice, which is why software uses IANA names like America/New_York (a database of history and future rules) rather than raw offsets. Three-letter abbreviations are traps: CST alone means Central, China, or Cuba Standard Time.

DST traps

Twice a year wall clocks lie: in spring an hour doesn't exist (02:30 on the spring-forward night never happens), in fall an hour happens twice (01:30 is ambiguous). Cron jobs in the gap get skipped or doubled, "add 24 hours" stops meaning "same time tomorrow", and any code that parses a local time without a zone rolls the dice twice a year. Schedule in UTC or pin an IANA zone.

The Y2038 problem

Signed 32-bit Unix time overflows at 2147483647 = 2038-01-19 03:14:07 UTC (try the example button) and wraps to December 1901. Modern systems moved to 64 bits, but embedded devices, old file formats, and packed binary protocols still carry 32-bit timestamps — the fix is deployment, not design.

Leap seconds & smearing

Earth's rotation drifts, so UTC occasionally inserts a leap second — and Unix time pretends it didn't happen: 23:59:60 simply doesn't exist in epoch arithmetic, so an epoch day is always exactly 86,400 s. Big fleets "smear" the extra second across hours instead of stepping. Two careful systems can legitimately disagree by a second — if that matters to you, you already know it.
