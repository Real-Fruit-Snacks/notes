Understanding certificates

PEM & DER

A PEM file is base64 armor: the BEGIN/END lines wrap a base64 payload, and the bytes inside are DER — a compact binary encoding of the certificate's fields. This page decodes the armor and walks the binary directly.

What's in a certificate

The signed part — the "to-be-signed" block — carries a serial number, the issuer's name, a validity window, the subject's name and the subject's public key. This tool reads exactly the fields it shows and skips the rest (extensions, signatures).

The validity window

notBefore and notAfter bound when the certificate may be trusted. RFC 5280 stores years up to 2049 as two digits (UTCTime) and 2050 onward as four (GeneralizedTime) — a quirk this parser handles. Verdicts compare against your computer's clock.

Why the CA's expiry matters too

Clients validate the whole chain: your client certificate and the CA that signed it must both be inside their validity windows. Long-lived OpenVPN setups often fail years later because the CA — not the client cert — expired. Check both cards.

Self-signed vs CA-issued

When a certificate's subject and issuer are identical, it signed itself — typical for a root CA. In an .ovpn file the <ca> block is usually self-signed and the <cert> block is issued by it; the self-signed tag tells them apart at a glance.

Private keys & this page

Private-key and tls-auth blocks have no expiry date, so this tool ignores them — it never parses, stores or displays them. Nothing you paste is uploaded anywhere: the page makes zero network requests, which you can confirm in your browser's DevTools Network tab.
